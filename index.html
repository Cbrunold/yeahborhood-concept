Sheet B

The whole UX is four dials

The giant category tree in the current UI is really one dial among four. Every screen in the app is nothing more than a setting of who you are, where you stand, when you're looking, and what you're after. That's the simplification that makes the interface teachable in ten seconds.

Dial 1 — Who's asking?

The Lens

Your verified relationship to the block changes what surfaces and what you can do. A newcomer sees onboarding; a business owner sees footfall.

Dial 2 — Where?

The Radius

Walking distance, built around you. Not a city, not a metro area — the block. Scale happens one zipcode at a time, never by zooming out.

The Timeline

The same map at three moments. Roll It Back is the feature none of the six inspirations have — the block's memory, all the way down.

Dial 4 — What for?

The Intent

Verbs, not categories. The taxonomy (groceries, fashion, saunas, tarot…) hides behind plain intents — the tree is for the database, not the user.

Eat & drinkDiscoverBuyLearnGet helpDo a good deedGet pampered

Examples: Newcomer + 5-min walk + HappenNow + Eat → "three counters locals actually queue at, one has a first-timer perk." Resident + block + Roll It Back + Learn → "your building in 1924, with sources."

And one input that isn't a dial, because nobody chooses it: conditions. Weather and time bias every ranking — rain due in 45 minutes pushes cafés up and parks down; sun reverses it; 11pm favors what's open. That's the context engine from the MVP brief: the same four dials, re-ranked by the sky.

Sheet C

Stolen well: six apps, one upgrade each

The savant mix isn't a feature pile — it's one borrowed trick per inspiration, each fixed with the thing they all lack: verification. That column is the pitch.

Borrowed fromTheir best trickThe Yeahborhood twist
Google Maps The map is the interface — spatial memory beats menus. The map is the home screen; every layer (knowledge, deals, events, history) renders on it. No separate "browse" mode.
Yelp Rich business pages people check before walking in. Reviews only from verified visits. Proof-of-patronage kills fake reviews; merchants pay for outcomes, not ads.
Foursquare The check-in habit; being somewhere is worth points. A check-in becomes an attestation — "the Yeah" — that settles real perks automatically. Mayor badges grow up into value.
Craigslist Zero-friction classifieds; anyone can post anything. The bulletin board keeps the friction-free posting, adds verified handoff — escrow releases when the deal provably happened.
Meetup Structured events with RSVPs and recurring groups. Verified attendance — organizers, sponsors and the city fund events per human who actually showed up.
Nextdoor Genuinely hyperlocal reach; neighbors can actually find each other. Identity-verified lenses plus the fact ledger under every claim. Signal without the paranoia-and-lost-cats noise floor.

Sheet G

Build order: earn each layer

The trap in the brief is building Layer 1 first. Nobody needs a trust protocol for a block that has no ledger and no users. Sequence it so every stage delivers value alone:

Ledger the two pilots: 10012 ✓ & 11213

Done for NoHo (July 2026): 1,170 buildings, 195 doors, 344 businesses — audited, deduped, enriched with live hours, websites and Place IDs, closures recorded as tenancy history. Crown Heights runs the same script pipeline next; its buildings import is free (same city open data).

Open the bulletin board

Marketplace + favors + surveys for the first 10 residents and providers. Craigslist-simple; verification is just "both parties tap confirm." Trust scores start accruing.

Ship the Yeah with a QR, not IoT

Three merchants, one perk each, a QR at the counter. Verified-visit perks prove the economic loop — merchant pays per outcome — with zero hardware. The MVP brief locks this in: QR check-ins become verification events, points settle off-chain, and weather-aware action cards are the daily hook. IoT and trusted hardware harden it later.

Fund one civic action

One cleanup or one class, paid per verified attendee by a BID or sponsor. This is the demo that turns the deck's "transparent community funding" into a receipt you can show a city.

Only now, the protocol

When one zipcode has a ledger, a marketplace, paying merchants and a funded civic loop — codify what already works into ZKB and open it to developers. The 10K-to-activate-a-zipcode franchise becomes the scaling story, not the starting point.
